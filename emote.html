EMOTE

Visual Shader for Non-Anthropomorphic Socio-Emotional Embodied Agents

Michael Sheroubi  |  February 2026    HCI Affective Computing NLP Unity / Real-Time

Overview

EMOTE is a procedurally generated visual shader that communicates the emotional state of a conversational AI agent through shape, motion, and color — without using a face. Grounded in perceptual psychology research on shape–emotion and color–emotion associations, the blob representation allows users to interpret affect while maintaining a clear boundary between AI and human interaction. The system runs end-to-end locally: Whisper for speech recognition, Gemma 3 (1B) via Ollama for language generation, a fine-tuned DistilBERT for emotion classification, and Piper for text-to-speech, all orchestrated by Unity.

1. Introduction & Motivation

Human beings have the tendency to form emotional attachments to non-emotional beings. As the use of LLM agents becomes increasingly integrated into everyday life, researchers face an important question: how should socio-emotional interactions with embodied agents be modelled?

While it might be tempting to give these agents realistic faces, the interaction remains unsettling until we exit the uncanny valley — and a fully realistic face unlocks a new set of risks if people unknowingly interact with AI models. We chose to explore how emotional communication can be integrated into conversational AI without relying on anthropomorphic realism.

The Uncanny Valley Problem (Mori, 1970): Near-human representations evoke discomfort rather than empathy. Fully realistic faces blur the distinction between human and artificial agents, raising concerns about transparency and emotional manipulation.

We intentionally designed a visualization that is expressive yet unmistakably artificial. The blob communicates emotional state through motion, color, and shape — allowing users to interpret affect without attributing human identity to the system. This is particularly relevant for applications involving children, education, or emotional-support interfaces, where emotional clarity and transparency are essential.

Context: Major AI labs (OpenAI, Google, Perplexity) have independently arrived at blob-like visualizations for their voice interaction UIs — suggesting convergent design intuition toward non-anthropomorphic affective displays.

2. Theoretical Foundation

Shape Selection: Kiki and Bouba

A growing body of perceptual psychology suggests that humans do not interpret shape as emotionally neutral. Vainio et al. (2025) study the association between emotions, shapes, and sounds through both explicit judgments and implicit association measures, reporting a robust mapping:

  • Angular / sharp forms → linked to anger and high-arousal negative affect
  • Rounded forms → linked to positive / calm affective interpretations

This is consistent with the broader kiki–bouba intuition [Ramachandran & Hubbard, 2001]: humans reliably match spiky shapes with "kiki"-like stimuli and rounded shapes with "bouba"-like stimuli — a cross-modal correspondence that appears early and is stable across contexts.

Color Selection: Do We Feel Colors?

Jonauskaite & Mohr (2025) conducted a systematic review of 132 studies spanning 1895–2022, involving over 42,266 participants from 64 countries, confirming robust color–emotion associations:

Color PropertyEmotional Association
Light colorsPositive valence
Dark colorsNegative valence
Warm hues (red, yellow, orange)High arousal / power
Cool hues (blue, green)Low arousal / calm
High saturationIntensity, excitement
Low saturation / greyNeutral to negative low arousal

These patterns hold across methods and cultures, positioning color as a reliable affective communication tool — though the associations are mostly correlative rather than causal.

3. The Blob — Mathematical Model

The core novelty of this project is EMOTE, a procedurally generated mesh that reacts to the semantic emotional content and acoustic properties of speech. The blob is constructed using concentric Unity LineRenderers, creating a depth field. The shape of each ring is defined by a polar coordinate system modulated by Perlin noise.

Radius Formula

For a given vertex at angle θ, the radius R is calculated as:

R(θ, t) = R_base + A_audio(t) + D_emotion · ( 2 · Noise(x, y) − 1 )
  • R_base — resting radius of the layer
  • A_audio(t) — audio reactivity term derived from the RMS amplitude of the current audio frame, boosted by a sensitivity factor k_lip
  • D_emotion — distortion amplitude for the current emotion (e.g., high for Anger, low for Sadness)
  • Noise(x, y) — sample from a 2D Perlin noise field

Coherent Noise Sampling

To ensure concentric layers move as a cohesive fluid entity rather than independent rings, noise coordinates are derived using a global phase φ(t) and a spatial offset δ_layer:

x = cos(θ) · S + δ_layer
y = sin(θ) · S + φ(t) − τ_lag

Where τ_lag introduces a temporal delay for outer rings, creating an outward ripple effect radiating from the center — giving the blob a fluid, organic feel.

Emotional Interpolation & Uncertainty

The system does not switch between discrete emotional states. Instead, it interpolates between a Neutral State (smooth, white, circular) and the predicted Emotional State based on the model's confidence C.

To prevent washed-out colors during transition, a non-linear boost curve is applied to color interpolation while keeping geometric transitions linear:

Color_final = Lerp( Color_neutral, Color_emotion, 1 − (1 − C)³ )
This ensures that even low-confidence predictions (C ≈ 0.3) result in perceptible color shifts, providing immediate visual feedback. The cubic easing curve front-loads the color transition rather than making it feel sluggish.

4. System Architecture

Unity acts as the central orchestrator for three asynchronous loops. All components run locally, built from scratch with no reference material — including the Whisper integration, Piper TTS server, emotion classifier, and local LLM.

🎙 Input Loop
Captures user audio via key-press trigger, buffers it, and transcribes using a local speech-to-text model (Whisper ggml-base, quantized).
🧠 Cognitive Loop
Generates textual responses via Gemma 3 (1B) through Ollama while simultaneously running DistilBERT emotion classification on the user's input.
🔊 Output Loop
Synthesizes speech via Piper TTS, streams audio to Unity, and modulates the EMOTE blob in real-time based on amplitude RMS + emotion probability distributions.

⚠ Primary latency bottleneck.

Speech-to-Text — Whisper

Speech recognition is handled by OpenAI's Whisper, specifically the quantized ggml-base model, integrated into Unity using the Whisper.Unity wrapper.

Generative Text — Gemma 3 (1B) via Ollama

Gemma 3 1B was selected as the heaviest model runnable locally without significantly hurting latency. The Unity client communicates via REST API calls, with a sliding window context buffer retaining the last 10 interactions for continuity.

Emotion Classification — Fine-tuned DistilBERT

Originally, a separate Gemma call was used for emotion classification. This was replaced with a fine-tuned DistilBERT model to get logits and probability distributions efficiently. DistilBERT was chosen for its significantly smaller size while retaining most of BERT's capabilities.

Training Details

  • Dataset: dair-ai/emotion — 16,000 labeled English tweets (Saravia et al., EMNLP 2018)
  • Classes: Sadness (0), Joy (1), Love (2), Anger (3), Fear (4), Surprise (5)
  • Loss: Cross-entropy on the classification head output

The fine-tuned model is deployed via a Flask Python server. It accepts text input and returns a probability distribution vector P = [p₀, p₁, …, p₅] where ∑P = 1. These probabilities drive the blob's visual layers.

Text-to-Speech — Piper

Piper was chosen for its speed and lightweight footprint. The en_US-lessac-medium ONNX model is used. The pipeline:

  1. Text is cleaned of non-alphanumeric artifacts.
  2. The Python server pipes the text to the Piper executable.
  3. Piper generates a raw 16-bit PCM WAV file.
  4. Unity receives the byte stream, parses the WAV header, and creates an AudioClip for playback.

5. Emotion Parameterization

The six emotion classes map directly to visual parameters grounded in the research reviewed in Section 2. The angularity/roundness axis (kiki–bouba) is encoded in the distortion amplitude — high distortion produces spiky, angular forms; low distortion produces smooth, round blobs.

The Fear blob uses an unusually large scale (10.0) with minimal distortion, producing a large but eerily still form — reflecting high arousal paired with freezing behavior rather than explosive motion.

Neutral state is represented by a smooth, white, circular form at rest. Surprise shares similar geometry but produces a transient high-brightness flash, distinguishing it through temporal dynamics rather than a static color difference.

The distinction between Neutral and Surprise cannot be captured in a still image — it is encoded in motion speed and the brightness flash, requiring live interaction to perceive.

6. User Study

A preliminary user study was conducted with N=12 colleagues (students, engineers, and scientists, aged 21–35). Discussion

The gap in emotion recognition speed and confidence between facial and blob conditions is unsurprising: humans have years of practice reading facial cues, whereas mappings from abstract shape and color to discrete emotion categories are less standardized and less familiar. Crucially, two participants who initially struggled with blob emotion recognition correctly classified both joy and anger after a single additional exposure — suggesting that blob-to-emotion associations may be learnable and not purely intuitive.

Key tension: Participants classified facial emotions with perfect accuracy and high confidence — but strongly rejected facial agents for interaction. Accuracy of emotion legibility is not the primary concern for user preference; anthropomorphic discomfort dominates.

7. Conclusion & Future Work

By encoding emotional state in a continuous space of shape, motion, and color, EMOTE achieves emotional legibility while avoiding the technical burden and ethical risks of anthropomorphic realism. The design choices are grounded in existing research: angularity vs. roundness as an affect cue, and the links between color properties (lightness, saturation, hue) and emotional valence/arousal.

The user study supports the central motivation: most participants preferred the blob representation, with several explicitly rejecting facial animation as unsettling. The recognition gap is noted — and the evidence that blob cues can be learned with minimal additional exposure suggests a promising direction.

Future Directions

  • Standardized visual vocabularies — Develop and test more consistent emotion cue sets. Just as humans learn to read non-verbal cues from each other, a standardized "blob vocabulary" adopted industry-wide would accelerate user intuition.
  • Longer-term learning studies — Measure how quickly users develop reliable blob-to-emotion mappings across extended interactions.
  • Depth model fusion — Combine VGGT's pose predictions with DA2's cleaner depth maps for richer visual output contexts.
  • Continuous emotion space — Move beyond six discrete classes toward a valence/arousal 2D plane, allowing more nuanced and blended emotional expressions.
  • Latency reduction — The output loop (TTS generation) is the primary bottleneck; exploring streaming TTS or on-device neural vocoders could dramatically improve interaction fluidity.
